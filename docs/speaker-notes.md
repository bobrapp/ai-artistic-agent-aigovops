# Speaker Notes: Kittitas Artist Business Coach — Board Pitch

## How to Use These Notes

These notes accompany the 13-slide pitch deck (`pitch.html`). Read the slide first, then use these notes to elaborate. Each section includes the core talking point, supporting details, and a suggested transition to the next slide. Allow approximately 20-25 minutes for the full presentation, leaving 10-15 minutes for Q&A.

---

## Slide 1: Title Slide

**"Kittitas Artist Business Coach: AI-Powered Support for Working Artists"**

**Opening:**
Start by asking the room a question: "How many of you know an artist in Kittitas County who is incredibly talented but struggles to make ends meet?" Pause. "That's what this is about."

The Kittitas Artist Business Coach is not about replacing human connection or arts programming. It's about solving a very specific, documented problem: artists have tremendous creative skill and zero business training, and there's currently no accessible way for them to get it.

**Transition:** "Let me show you what the problem actually looks like."

---

## Slide 2: The Problem

**Core talking point:** The skills gap is real, it's measurable, and it's not the artists' fault.

Artists are trained to make art. Business school is not part of an MFA. The result is that talented people price their work based on gut feeling, sign contracts they don't understand, miss tax deductions they're entitled to, and eventually burn out or give up — not because they stopped loving their work, but because they couldn't make it pay.

**Key statistics to cite:**
- 71% of self-employed artists cite not knowing how to price their work as a top barrier to sustainable income (CreativePulse, 2021)
- Artists with business training earn 34% more on average within two years (Americans for the Arts, 2022)
- Rural artists are significantly less likely to have business licenses, contracts, or retirement savings — not because they don't care, but because access to help is limited (WESTAF, 2020)

**Local grounding:** "We're not talking about abstract national statistics. We're talking about the painters, ceramicists, photographers, and musicians in Ellensburg, Cle Elum, and Roslyn. They are us. They are our neighbors."

**Transition:** "So what do we propose to do about it?"

---

## Slide 3: The Solution

**Core talking point:** A 24/7 AI business coach, built for artists, tuned for Washington state.

The Kittitas Artist Business Coach is an AI system — think of it like a very knowledgeable friend who happens to know everything about small business law in Washington, arts-specific tax rules, pricing strategy, and contracts. Available at 2am when the artist is finishing a commission and wondering if they should have a contract. Available on a Sunday when they're pricing work for a show. Available without judgment when they're embarrassed to admit they've been operating without a business license for three years.

**What it is NOT:**
- It is not a generative art tool. It does not make images or music.
- It is not replacing human advisors. It is a first responder that knows when to hand off to a CPA, an attorney, or an SBDC advisor.
- It is not a data harvesting operation. Artist conversations are not used for training.

**Transition:** "Let me show you what it actually looks like to use it."

---

## Slide 4: The Living Curriculum

**Core talking point:** 15 conversational modules covering the complete business lifecycle of an artist.

The curriculum is "living" for two reasons: it's delivered conversationally (not as a video to watch passively), and it's updated as laws and best practices change. Washington's B&O tax rules, for example, changed in 2024 — a static PDF curriculum would be wrong. The Living Curriculum gets updated.

**Module overview (walk through the 15 topics quickly):**
1. Hobby vs. business — understanding the IRS distinction
2. Registering in Washington — UBI, city licenses, DBA
3. Sole prop vs. LLC vs. S-Corp — choosing the right structure
4. Pricing your work — the real math, not just "what feels right"
5. Bookkeeping in 30 minutes a month — seriously, that's all it takes
6. Federal taxes — Schedule C, quarterly estimated taxes, deductions
7. Washington state taxes — B&O, sales tax, use tax
8. Contracts every artist needs — commissions, consignment, licensing
9. Copyright and trademark — what artists own and how to protect it
10. Portfolio and website — professional digital presence
11. Email and Instagram that sell — marketing that doesn't feel gross
12. Galleries and consignment — how to negotiate, what to watch for
13. Selling online — Etsy, own website, platforms compared
14. Art fairs and festivals — booth fees, sales tax, logistics
15. Health, retirement, and longevity — sustainable career planning

**Key point:** Each module ends with a concrete next step. Learning without action is forgotten. The coach always leaves the artist with something to do this week.

**Transition:** "This isn't theoretical. Let me show you a conversation."

## Slide 5: Demo — Conversation Mockup

**Core talking point:** The coach sounds like a knowledgeable friend, not a legal disclaimer.

Walk the audience through 1-2 of the demo conversations from the `demo.html` file. Suggested demos:

**Demo 1 — Pricing (most relatable):**
Show how the coach walks an artist through the actual cost-of-goods and hourly-rate calculation, and gently pushes back when the artist underprices. This is the most common pain point and the most immediately impactful module.

**Demo 2 — Washington B&O Tax:**
Show how the coach explains the B&O tax in plain English, identifies the artist's likely classification (Services, at 1.5%), and explains quarterly filing. This is the most common "I didn't know I was supposed to be doing this" moment for artists.

**Tone note:** The coach uses direct, warm, non-condescending language. It never says "you should have known this." It starts from "you're here now, let's get you set up correctly."

**Transition:** "This is what we want to pilot. Here's what that looks like."

## Slide 6: The Pilot Plan

**Core talking point:** Small, affordable, measurable. 10-20 artists over 8 weeks.

**Pilot structure:**
- Partner with Gallery One as recruitment hub (they know the artist community)
- Recruit 10-20 artists across skill levels and career stages
- 8-week guided cohort with optional in-person kickoff and closing session
- Pre/post survey measuring confidence, income tracking, and specific actions taken

**Why Gallery One:** They are already the trusted gathering point for working artists in Ellensburg. They have the relationships. They have the space. This is a natural partnership.

**What success looks like at 8 weeks:**
- 70%+ completion rate (for a free program, this would be excellent)
- Measurable confidence increase in at least 5 business topics
- At least 50% of participants take one concrete business action (license, contract, price revision)
- Qualitative feedback sufficient to refine the curriculum

**Transition:** "What does this cost?"

## Slide 7: The Economics

**Core talking point:** Remarkably affordable. ~$50/month operating cost.

**Cost breakdown:**
- AI platform (Claude API via Hyperagent): ~$30-40/month at pilot scale
- Domain and hosting: ~$10/month
- Staff time: 2-4 hours/month for curriculum updates and moderation
- **Total operating cost: ~$50/month, or $600/year**

**Pilot budget (6 months):**
- Staff coordination (10 hours @ $45/hr): $450
- Marketing and recruitment materials: $200
- Kickoff event (space, refreshments): $200
- Contingency (10%): $85
- **Pilot total: ~$935-1,080**

**Comparison:**
- A single SBDC advisor session is free but books 2-3 weeks out and covers general business, not arts-specific topics
- A private business consultant charges $150-300/hour
- The coach is available 24/7 at $50/month for unlimited users

**Long-term sustainability:** After the pilot, the annual sustaining cost is $700-1,200/year — within reach of a single small grant or a modest line item in the KCAC budget.

## Slide 8: Global Landscape

**Core talking point:** No one is doing this for rural artists. We have a chance to build the model.

**What exists nationally:**
- Fractured Atlas: Excellent, but New York-centric
- Artist Trust: Washington's best, but Seattle-centric
- SBDC: Good for general business, not arts-specific
- Montana Arts Council: Rural-focused, but no AI delivery
- NEA/ArtsWA: Organization-facing, rarely reaches individual rural artists

**The gap:** No program combines rural focus, Washington state specificity, AI delivery, asynchronous access, and local community context. The Kittitas Artist Business Coach fills that gap.

**The replication opportunity:** If Kittitas builds this and documents what works, every rural arts organization in the Pacific Northwest can adapt it. KCAC has the opportunity to be the model, not just the beneficiary.

**Transition:** "What are the concerns? Let's address them directly."

## Slide 9: Addressing Concerns

**Core talking point:** The arts community has legitimate AI concerns. Here's how we've addressed them.

Be direct and honest about this slide. Don't minimize the concerns.

**Concern 1: "AI took our images / stole our work"**
Response: This tool does not generate images, music, or creative work. It is a business advisor. The AI in this coach has not been trained on artist work. These are categorically different applications of AI technology.

**Concern 2: "It will replace human advisors and arts administrators"**
Response: The explicit design of this tool is to know when to say "this is beyond my scope, please call your accountant." Every module has escalation triggers. The goal is to increase demand for human professionals, not replace them — because artists who understand their own situations are better clients for accountants and attorneys.

**Concern 3: "AI makes stuff up — what if it gives wrong advice?"**
Response: Every module includes clear disclaimers. The curriculum covers well-established, stable business concepts. The coach is designed to be conservative: when in doubt, it says "consult a professional" rather than speculating. We review and update the curriculum regularly.

**Concern 4: "What about privacy?"**
Response: Artist conversations are not used for training. The platform uses standard commercial data handling. We can discuss specific data policies if the board wants to review them.

## Slide 12: Why This Matters

**Core talking point:** Artist economic sustainability is community sustainability.

Artists are not just cultural ornaments. They are small business owners, employers, tourists attractions, and community anchors. When artists can't sustain themselves, they leave or stop. The artistic character of Kittitas County — which drives tourism, attracts residents, and defines quality of life — depends on artists being able to make a living here.

The Kittitas Artist Business Coach is not a charity. It's infrastructure. It's the equivalent of the road that gets the artist to the market, the accountant who keeps their lights on, the contract that protects their work. We're just making that infrastructure available to everyone, not just those who can afford it or who happen to know the right people.

## Anticipated Q&A

### "What if the AI gives bad advice?"

This is the most important question to answer well. Acknowledge it seriously: "That's the right thing to worry about." Then explain:

1. The curriculum covers well-established, stable concepts — not edge cases
2. Every module has explicit escalation triggers ("consult a tax professional for your specific situation")
3. The coach is designed to be conservative: when uncertain, it defers to professionals
4. We review the curriculum quarterly for accuracy
5. The same risk exists with Google, YouTube, or any other information source — and those sources are less arts-specific and less likely to flag their limitations

### "How do we know artists will actually use it?"

Point to the Montana Arts Council data: asynchronous digital resources are accessed 6x more than live workshops in rural settings. Artists have irregular schedules. A tool available at 2am when they're finishing a commission is more useful than a workshop they couldn't attend.

Also: the cohort model with Gallery One creates social accountability. Artists are more likely to engage when their peers are engaging alongside them.

### "What happens after the pilot funding runs out?"

The annual sustaining cost is $700-1,200/year — small enough to be a line item in the KCAC budget or covered by a single small grant. We'll use the pilot data to build a grant application to ArtsWA, the NEA, or foundation sources. The documented outcomes (artist income data, completion rates, specific actions taken) are exactly what funders want to see.

### "Could Central Washington University be involved?"

Yes — and this could be a great partnership. CWU's art department could contribute curriculum review expertise. Their business department could provide student volunteers for program support. This is worth exploring after the pilot.

### "Is this replicable elsewhere?"

Absolutely, and intentionally. Everything we build will be documented. The curriculum will be open-source. The playbook for running a rural artist business coach pilot will be freely available. Kittitas can be the model that Yakima, Chelan, and Okanogan counties adapt — and eventually rural arts organizations nationally.
