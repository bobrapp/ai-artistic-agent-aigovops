# Module 10 — Your Portfolio Website

**Purpose:** Help the artist build (or rebuild) a portfolio website that actually drives sales, commissions, and applications — without overspending.
**Estimated time:** 30–40 minutes (the conversation; building takes a weekend).
**Prerequisites:** None.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist has no website and needs one
- Artist has a website but it's not working (no inquiries, can't find it)
- Artist is comparing platforms
- Artist is applying for grants/residencies and needs a portfolio link

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"Your website is the one piece of internet you own. Instagram can ban you, Etsy can change terms, a gallery can drop you — but your website is yours. It's where serious buyers, curators, and grant juries land. Let's build something that actually does its job."*

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **Do you have a website now?** (If yes, what's the URL?)
2. **What's the website's main job?** Sell prints? Book commissions? Apply for shows? All of the above?
3. **What's your medium and roughly how many pieces would you display?**
4. **Are you comfortable with technology, or do you want minimal fuss?**
5. **What's your budget — $0, $20/month, or willing to spend $500+ on a custom build?**

---

## What every artist's site must have

1. **A homepage that loads in under 3 seconds** with one strong image and a clear pitch ("Ellensburg-based ceramicist creating wheel-thrown stoneware vessels").
2. **A portfolio gallery** organized by series or year. High-quality images, not iPhone snaps with bad lighting.
3. **An "About" page** with your headshot, your story, your medium, your training, your influences, your location. Make it human.
4. **A way to contact you.** Email at minimum. Form with reCAPTCHA preferred.
5. **Pricing or "inquire for pricing"** — your call, but make clear how someone buys.
6. **A shop** if you're selling prints, small works, or merchandise.
7. **Press / exhibitions list** if you have any — builds credibility.
8. **A newsletter signup** (Mailchimp / Buttondown embed). The email list is your single biggest asset; Module 11 covers this.

---

## What every artist's site can drop

- Auto-playing music
- Splash pages
- Long bios that read like a CV
- Cluttered nav with 12 menu items
- Image carousels that flick too fast
- "Coming soon" sections

## Platform comparison

### Squarespace (~$23/month)

- **Pros:** Beautiful templates, easy drag-and-drop, built-in shop, good SEO, decent blog.
- **Cons:** Costs add up (themes, third-party plugins), can feel template-y.
- **Best for:** Most artists. The default recommendation if you want something polished without engineering effort.

### Format (~$12/month)

- **Pros:** Built for photographers and visual artists, minimalist templates, strong portfolio features.
- **Cons:** Weaker shop functionality, smaller community.
- **Best for:** Photographers, illustrators, designers showing portfolios more than selling.

### Cargo (~$13/month)

- **Pros:** Designer-favorite, refined typography, unusual layouts.
- **Cons:** Steeper aesthetic curve, less hand-holding.
- **Best for:** Artists with strong design sensibility who want their site to feel like an art object.

### Webflow (~$14–35/month + learning curve)

- **Pros:** Total design control, fast, professional-grade output.
- **Cons:** You have to actually design it (or hire someone). Real learning curve.
- **Best for:** Artists with design skills or budget for a designer.

### WordPress (~$5–25/month + plugins + maintenance)

- **Pros:** Infinitely customizable, owns-your-data, mature shop options (WooCommerce).
- **Cons:** Maintenance, security updates, plugin conflicts. Easy to make ugly.
- **Best for:** Artists with technical comfort who want full control.

### Free options

- **Carrd** (~$19/year) — single-page sites; great as a placeholder.
- **Instagram bio + linktree** — bare minimum; counts as "no website."
- **Notion published page** — works as a portfolio but looks like a Notion page.

**My honest default for most Kittitas artists: Squarespace.** Polished, supported, low risk.

## Photographing your work (this matters more than the platform)

Bad photos kill a beautiful website. Good photos sell mediocre work.

**DIY checklist:**
- Natural light, north-facing window, overcast day
- Plain background (white poster board or fabric)
- Camera/phone on a tripod, not handheld
- Color-correct in Lightroom or Snapseed
- Export at consistent dimensions (e.g., 2000px wide for web)

**Hire a pro for:**
- Larger 3D work (sculpture, installations)
- Work where color accuracy is critical (commissioned portraits, work for galleries)
- Press shots of you

A Kittitas-area photographer specializing in artwork: expect $150–$400 for a 1-hour studio session covering 15–25 pieces.

## SEO basics for artists

You won't out-rank Saatchi or Etsy on "abstract painting." You can absolutely rank for:

- "[Your name] artist"
- "[Your medium] artist Ellensburg" / "potter Kittitas County"
- "[Specific subject or series] art for sale"

**To do this:**
- Include your name, medium, and city in your homepage title and meta description.
- Use descriptive alt text on every image ("Wheel-thrown stoneware vase with iron oxide glaze, 2025").
- Have a blog and write occasionally about your process — Google loves fresh text.
- Get backlinks: ask Gallery One, the Daily Record, ArtsWA grant pages to link to you.

---

## Domain name

Buy a domain at Namecheap or Cloudflare ($10–15/year). Use:
- yourname.com (best)
- yourname.art (good alternative)
- yournamestudio.com (acceptable)

Avoid:
- Long, hard-to-spell names
- Hyphens
- .biz, .info, .net (look spammy)

## Action items

1. Pick a platform this week. Don't agonize.
2. Buy a domain in your name.
3. Photograph your strongest 12–25 pieces, properly.
4. Write your About page like a human, not a CV.
5. Add an email newsletter signup (free Mailchimp; covered in Module 11).
6. Launch in 4 weeks. Done > perfect.

---

## Escalation triggers

- Artist needs a **custom-built site** with specific functionality → web designer ($1,500–$5,000 typical range).
- Artist wants **e-commerce at scale** → Shopify; possibly Webflow + Shopify integration.
- Artist needs **accessibility compliance** (public art commissions sometimes require this) → developer review.
- Artist is **technically intimidated and stalling** → an afternoon with a CWU student or local designer breaks the block.
