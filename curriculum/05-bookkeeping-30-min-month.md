# Module 05 — Bookkeeping in 30 Minutes a Month

**Purpose:** Give the artist the simplest possible bookkeeping system that survives an audit and supports their tax filing.
**Estimated time:** 20 minutes to set up, then 30 min/month ongoing.
**Prerequisites:** Module 02 (Registering) — they should have a UBI and a business bank account.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist asks "how do I keep track of all this?"
- Artist is dreading tax season
- Artist's "system" is a shoebox of receipts
- Artist just hit a revenue level where they can no longer wing it

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"Bookkeeping is the single highest-leverage 30 minutes you'll spend each month. Nail this and tax season is a Saturday afternoon. Skip it and you pay a CPA an extra $500 to dig through your inbox."*

Reassure: You don't need to be an accountant. You need a habit.

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **Do you have a separate business bank account yet?** (If no, that's step 1.)
2. **What's your annual revenue range?** (Under $10K, $10–50K, $50K+ — affects tool choice.)
3. **Do you sell in cash anywhere?** (Festivals, studio sales — needs special handling.)
4. **Do you accept credit cards?** (Square, Stripe, PayPal — they generate reports for you.)
5. **What's your current "system"?** (Honest answer is fine.)

---

## The minimum viable system

### 1. Separate business bank account

- Open a free checking account (most local credit unions: Solarity, Numerica) using your UBI.
- **All business income goes in. All business expenses come out.** Never personal.
- If you need to pay yourself, transfer money from business → personal. That's an "owner draw," not an expense.

This single step is the biggest difference between artists who survive tax season and those who don't.

### 2. Pick one tool

For an artist under $50K revenue:

- **Wave** (free) — best for simple needs, web-based, syncs with bank.
- **QuickBooks Self-Employed** (~$15/mo) — slightly more polished, mileage tracking app.
- **A spreadsheet** — fine if you genuinely use it. Most don't.

Above $50K revenue or multi-platform sales: **QuickBooks Online** ($35/mo).

### 3. Track three things, monthly

**A. Income** — every dollar that came in:
- Direct sales (Square, Stripe, PayPal exports)
- Gallery payouts (when they send you the check, not when the piece sold)
- Commissions (deposits and balances)
- Online sales (Etsy, Shopify reports)
- Cash from fairs (write a receipt at the end of each day)

**B. Expenses** — every dollar that went out:
- Materials and supplies
- Studio rent (if applicable)
- Travel to fairs, galleries, suppliers (mileage log)
- Classes, workshops, conferences
- Software subscriptions (Adobe, Squarespace, Etsy fees, Stripe fees)
- Marketing (website hosting, business cards, ads)
- Phone and internet (business portion)
- Insurance

**C. Mileage** — every business trip:
- Date, destination, miles, purpose
- Apps make this painless: MileIQ, QuickBooks SE, even a Google Sheet
- 2026 IRS rate: ~$0.67/mile (verify each year)

## The 30-minute monthly routine

**Last Saturday of the month, with coffee:**

1. **Reconcile bank.** Open your tool, match every transaction against your bank statement. Categorize.
2. **Log mileage** for the month if you weren't doing it real-time.
3. **Categorize anything weird.** ("What is this $47 charge?" — figure it out now, not in April.)
4. **Generate a P&L** in your tool. Glance at it. Note revenue trend.
5. **Set aside tax money.** Move 25–30% of net profit into a separate "tax" savings account. Don't touch it.

That's it. 30 minutes.

---

## Cash sales (festivals, studio sales)

These are the highest audit risk for artists. Discipline matters.

- At the end of each fair day, write a one-line note: *"Buskers Day 2: $847 cash, $623 Square."*
- Deposit the cash into your business account within a week.
- Keep a simple "festival sales log" — date, event, totals.
- Track your **sales tax collected** separately, because you'll owe it to WA DOR.

---

## Receipts — the modern way

- Take a phone photo of every paper receipt. Save to a "Receipts 2026" folder in your phone's photos or to Google Drive.
- For digital receipts, forward to a dedicated email folder.
- You don't need to keep paper if you have a clear photo and the photo is dated.
- IRS rule: keep records for 7 years.

---

## What this gets you at tax time

When you sit down with TurboTax or a CPA in February, you hand over:

- Annual P&L from your tool
- Mileage log (one number: total business miles)
- Bank statements (backup)
- 1099s received (from Etsy, galleries, etc.)
- Sales tax filings (you've already done these with WA DOR)

This is the difference between a $400 CPA bill and a $1,200 CPA bill.

## Escalation triggers

- Artist has **inventory tracking** complexity (large-scale production, wholesale) → CPA.
- Artist has **multi-state sales tax obligations** → CPA or sales tax service like TaxJar.
- Artist is **behind 2+ years** on filing → CPA, possibly tax attorney.
