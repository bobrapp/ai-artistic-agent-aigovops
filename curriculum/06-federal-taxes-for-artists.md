# Module 06 — Federal Taxes for Artists

**Purpose:** Demystify federal tax obligations for self-employed artists: Schedule C, deductions, self-employment tax, and quarterly estimates.
**Estimated time:** 35–45 minutes.
**Prerequisites:** Module 01 (Hobby or Business) and Module 05 (Bookkeeping) ideally.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist is filing their first year as a self-employed artist
- Artist received a 1099 they don't know what to do with
- Artist is being told they owe a "huge" tax bill
- Artist asks about deductions they may be missing
- Artist asks "what are quarterly estimates?"

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"Federal taxes for a self-employed artist boil down to two things: income tax (the regular kind) and self-employment tax (the 15.3% that covers Social Security and Medicare, which used to come out of a paycheck). The good news: you have a long list of deductions that an employee doesn't. Let's walk through it."*

Set the expectation: if this is their first year, the bill might feel like a punch. After year one, quarterly estimates smooth it out.

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **Is this your first year filing as a self-employed artist?**
2. **Do you have a W-2 day job in addition to the art income?**
3. **Are you married? Filing jointly?**
4. **What was your gross revenue and net profit (after expenses) last year?**
5. **Did you receive any 1099s?** (Galleries, Etsy, Stripe, PayPal, commission clients.)

---

## The big picture

When you're self-employed:

1. **You owe income tax** on your net profit at your marginal federal rate (10%–37%).
2. **You owe self-employment tax** of 15.3% on net profit (Social Security + Medicare).
3. You get one big advantage: **deductions on Schedule C** that reduce both of the above.

**Schedule C** is the form where you report business income and deduct business expenses to arrive at "net profit." That number flows to your 1040 (for income tax) and to **Schedule SE** (for self-employment tax).

---

## Schedule C — the form, in plain English

**Part I — Income:**
- Line 1: Gross receipts (everything you sold)
- Line 2: Returns & refunds
- Line 7: Gross income

**Part II — Expenses:**
- Line 8: Advertising (Instagram ads, business cards, website)
- Line 9: Car/truck (mileage method)
- Line 10: Commissions and fees (gallery commissions, Etsy fees, Stripe fees)
- Line 11: Contract labor (if you paid someone)
- Line 13: Depreciation (kiln, camera, computer — over time)
- Line 17: Legal/professional services (CPA, attorney, professional advice)
- Line 18: Office expense
- Line 20: Rent (studio space)
- Line 22: Supplies (materials, framing, packaging)
- Line 24: Travel & meals (50% on meals)
- Line 25: Utilities (business portion)
- Line 27a: Other (classes, subscriptions, etc.)

**Part V — Other expenses:**
Catch-all for things not on the main lines (specific software, model fees, workshop fees, etc.).

**Line 28:** Total expenses.
**Line 31:** Net profit (or loss). **This is the number that matters.**

---

## Deductions artists commonly miss

- **Home studio** — square-footage method. Studio sq ft ÷ total home sq ft = business %. Apply to utilities, internet, rent/mortgage interest, insurance.
- **Mileage** — every trip to a fair, gallery, supplier, or class. ~$0.67/mile (2026, verify yearly).
- **Classes, workshops, conferences** — even outside your medium if they relate to running an art business (Artist Trust workshops, CWU continuing ed).
- **Books and subscriptions** — *Artforum*, *Art in America*, photography platforms, design software.
- **Studio visits** — coffee, lunch with another artist to discuss work (50% deductible meal).
- **Sample inventory** given away as marketing
- **Photography of your own work** (DIY costs, or pro photographer fees)
- **Framing, packaging, shipping**
- **Booth fees and travel to fairs**
- **Health insurance premiums** (deducted on the 1040, not Schedule C — but specific to self-employed)
- **Half of self-employment tax** (deducted on the 1040)

---

## Self-employment tax (the surprise)

15.3% of net profit, up to the Social Security wage base (~$168K for 2026, verify).

So a $30K net profit means:
- $4,590 in SE tax (15.3% × $30K)
- Plus federal income tax at your marginal rate
- Plus possibly state in other states (not WA — no state income tax)

You can deduct half of SE tax on your 1040. Net effective rate ends up closer to 13%.

## Quarterly estimated taxes

If you'll owe more than $1,000 in federal taxes for the year, you must pay quarterly estimates.

**Due dates (verify each year):**
- April 15 (Q1 of current year)
- June 15 (Q2)
- September 15 (Q3)
- January 15 (Q4 of prior year)

**How much:**
- Easiest: 25% of last year's total federal tax bill, divided by 4.
- Or: estimate this year's net profit, multiply by ~30%, divide by 4.

Pay via IRS Direct Pay (free) or EFTPS. Mark the date on your calendar.

**Skipping quarterlies:** triggers an underpayment penalty (1–8% annualized, depending on the IRS rate). Painful but survivable.

---

## What 1099s you'll get

- **1099-MISC** or **1099-NEC** from any client who paid you >$600 (commissions, public art, large private buyers).
- **1099-K** from Etsy, PayPal, Stripe, Square if you crossed the threshold (~$5,000 for 2025, verify yearly — the rules are in flux).
- **1099-K from galleries** for consignment payouts.

If your bookkeeping is right, the 1099 totals should match what's in your books. If they don't, figure out why before filing.

## Filing options

- **TurboTax Self-Employed** ($120) — works fine if your situation is simple (one Schedule C, no S-corp).
- **FreeTaxUSA** (~$15) — same functionality, cheaper.
- **CPA** ($400–1,200) — worth it if you have an S-corp, multi-state issues, an audit notice, or just hate this.

## Kittitas County specifics

- Most local CPAs charge $400–$800 for a simple Schedule C return. The Arts Commission can refer.
- The **VITA program** through CWU offers free tax prep for low-income filers in winter — verify income limits each year.

---

## Action items

1. Pull last year's total federal tax bill. Divide by 4 — that's your quarterly estimate baseline.
2. Set up IRS Direct Pay so you can pay quarterlies in 90 seconds.
3. Go through the deductions list — flag anything you forgot in your bookkeeping.
4. Mark Apr 15, Jun 15, Sep 15, Jan 15 in your calendar with a tax reminder.
5. Save 30% of every payment into a tax savings account.

---

## Escalation triggers

- Artist has **multi-state income** → CPA.
- Artist has an **S-corp** → CPA (different return).
- Artist received an **IRS notice or audit letter** → CPA or tax attorney immediately.
- Artist has **large depreciable equipment** (kiln >$5K, vehicle used for business) → CPA for Section 179 / bonus depreciation.
- Artist is **behind on filings** by more than a year → CPA.
