# Module 12 — Galleries & Consignment

**Purpose:** Demystify the gallery system: how consignment works, the math, finding the right gallery, reading contracts, and knowing when to end a relationship.
**Estimated time:** 30–40 minutes.
**Prerequisites:** Module 04 (Pricing) and Module 08 (Contracts) helpful.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist is being invited to consign with a gallery for the first time
- Artist is evaluating multiple galleries
- Artist has work in a gallery that isn't selling
- Artist is being asked to pay to show ("vanity gallery" alarm)
- Artist asks "is it worth it?"

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"Galleries are partners, not gatekeepers. The good ones bring you collectors you'd never find on your own. The bad ones tie up your inventory while doing nothing. Let me walk you through how this actually works, and how to tell which kind you're dealing with."*

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **Where is the gallery?** (Local, regional, urban.)
2. **What's their commission rate?**
3. **What term are they asking for?** (3 months? 12 months? Indefinite?)
4. **Have they sold work by artists at your career stage?**
5. **Are they asking you to pay anything upfront?** (Red flag if yes.)

---

## How consignment actually works

You retain ownership of the work while it sits in the gallery. The gallery is the agent; you are the principal. When a piece sells:

- The gallery collects payment from the buyer.
- The gallery deducts their commission.
- The gallery pays you the balance, typically within 30 days of the sale.

You still own the piece until it sells. If the gallery closes, your work belongs to you (this is why proper consignment contracts matter — see Module 08).

---

## The math

**A typical Pacific Northwest mid-tier gallery commission: 40–50%**.

- You sell a piece direct for $800 → you net $800.
- You sell a piece through a 50% gallery for $800 → you net $400.
- You sell a piece through a 40% gallery for $800 → you net $480.

So why do galleries make sense?

- They bring buyers you wouldn't reach: collectors, designers, interior designers, regional travelers.
- They provide credibility (CVs, press, future opportunities).
- They handle the sale (you don't have a conversation with every buyer).
- They take the marketing burden.

**Whether it's worth it = does the gallery move enough volume that 50% of more sales > 100% of fewer sales?**

A gallery that sells 3 pieces a year of yours at $800 each = $1,200 to you. If you'd otherwise sell 1 piece a year direct at $800 = $800 to you. Math works.

A gallery that sells 0 pieces in 12 months while holding $4,000 of your inventory = costs you everything you could have done with that inventory. Math doesn't work.

## Gallery tiers (general framework)

### Tier 1: Vanity galleries / pay-to-play

- They charge artists rental, hanging fees, or "exhibition fees."
- They don't curate or sell work — they rent wall space.
- **Avoid.** Some are technically not scams (you can hang on their wall) but they don't move work.
- Red flag: "We need $500 to cover printing for the exhibition."

### Tier 2: Cooperative galleries

- Artists are members; share rent, gallery duty, and curation.
- Often charge monthly dues ($30–200) plus commission (10–30%).
- Useful as a way to show consistently and build local presence.
- Watch out for: poorly run co-ops that take effort without generating sales.

### Tier 3: Regional commercial galleries

- Take consignment at 40–50%.
- Curate exhibitions, have a collector base, market the artists they represent.
- **Most artists in Kittitas County should be aiming for this tier.**
- Examples in the region: Gallery One (Ellensburg), galleries in Yakima, Wenatchee, Seattle's Pioneer Square neighborhood, Tacoma.

### Tier 4: Urban / blue-chip galleries

- Higher commission (50%), serious collector base, press coverage.
- Selective; usually require a CV with strong exhibitions and gallery placements.
- For most Kittitas artists, this is a 5–10 year arc, not a starting point.

## How to approach a gallery

1. **Research them first.** Visit. Look at their roster. Confirm they show your medium and aesthetic.
2. **Prepare a clean portfolio.** 10–15 strong recent works, well-photographed, with sizes and prices. PDF or website.
3. **Send a short email.** Three paragraphs: who you are, why this gallery specifically, attached portfolio. Subject line: "Submission: [Your Name], [medium]."
4. **Don't expect a reply.** Many galleries don't respond. Move on.
5. **In-person is better.** First Friday Art Walks are an excuse to meet gallerists. Bring cards, mention you'd love to send a portfolio later.
6. **Group shows are an on-ramp.** Many galleries will include emerging artists in themed group shows before representing them solo.

## Reading a consignment contract (the must-haves from Module 08)

- **Commission %:** 30–50% is normal. Question >50%.
- **Term:** 3–12 months with mutual termination. Beware indefinite or auto-renewal without notice.
- **Payment schedule:** Within 30 days of the sale. 60+ is concerning.
- **Pricing control:** You set prices. Gallery proposes discounts but you must approve.
- **Insurance:** Gallery insures consigned work.
- **Exclusivity:** Local exclusivity is acceptable. National exclusivity for a regional gallery is overreach.
- **Sales reporting:** Monthly statements of inventory and sales.
- **Reproduction rights:** Gallery uses images for promotion; doesn't gain general reproduction rights.

---

## Red flags

- "We need an upfront fee."
- "Indefinite term, no termination clause."
- "We don't insure the work."
- "We pay artists when we feel like it" / no clear schedule.
- "We can discount up to 50% without your approval."
- "We retain commission on all sales by the artist for 12 months after termination, regardless of source." (Sometimes legitimate for buyers they introduced; suspect when blanket.)
- Vague or hand-written contracts.

---

## Ending a relationship

It's fine to leave a gallery. Reasons:

- They're not selling.
- They're slow paying or not paying.
- Your work has evolved past their market.
- A better fit gallery wants to represent you and is asking for exclusivity.

**Process:**

1. Send written notice per the contract term (usually 30–60 days).
2. Schedule pickup of unsold inventory.
3. Reconcile any pending payments.
4. Don't bridge-burn — galleries talk to each other.

- **Gallery One Visual Arts Center** is a nonprofit with consignment programs and exhibitions. They actively support local artists; their terms tend to be artist-friendly. Confirm current commission rate directly.
- **Clymer Museum & Gallery** focuses on a specific aesthetic (Western, illustrative) but has occasional rotating exhibitions.

## Action items

1. List the galleries within 90 minutes that show your medium.
2. Visit at least three in person before submitting anywhere.
3. Build a clean 12-piece portfolio with dimensions and prices.
4. Draft a short submission email template.
5. If you're already in a gallery, audit: sales volume, payment timeliness, what they've actively done for you.

---

## Escalation triggers

- Contract review needed for **multi-year representation** → attorney.
- Unpaid balances or **disputes over consigned work** → attorney + check Module 09.
- Considering **multi-gallery representation across regions** → may need consistent pricing strategy; talk to a mentor or Artist Trust advisor.
- **Vanity gallery scam suspected** → flag with the Arts Commission; they can warn other local artists.
