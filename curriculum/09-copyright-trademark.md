# Module 09 — Copyright, Trademark, and Protecting Your Work

**Purpose:** Demystify copyright (automatic, but registration adds power), trademark (for studio/brand names), and the practical realities of protecting an art practice.
**Estimated time:** 25–30 minutes.
**Prerequisites:** None. Pairs well with Module 08 (Contracts).

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist asks "do I need to copyright this?"
- Artist found someone using their work on a t-shirt / Instagram / a stranger's wall
- Artist is about to publish, exhibit, or sell prints
- Artist is naming a studio and wants to protect the name
- Artist is licensing work for the first time

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"Copyright is automatic. The moment you create something — finish that painting, post that photo, hit save on that digital file — you own the copyright. You don't need to do anything. Registration is a separate thing that gives you stronger enforcement powers if something goes wrong. Let me walk through the difference, and what you actually need to do."*

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **What's the work?** (Original art, derivative, commissioned, photograph of someone else's work.)
2. **Where will it appear?** (Gallery, prints, online, licensing.)
3. **Has it already been copied without permission?**
4. **Do you have a studio or brand name you want to protect?**

---

## Copyright: the rules in 90 seconds

- **You own copyright automatically** the moment the work is fixed in a tangible form. Finished painting, saved photo, recorded performance, written manuscript.
- **Copyright lasts** your lifetime + 70 years (US).
- **You can give it away or license it** in writing.
- **Without registration**, you can still send takedown notices and demand someone stop. You **cannot sue for statutory damages or attorney's fees** — only actual damages, which are usually trivial for art.
- **With registration** (US Copyright Office, ~$45–65 per work or group registration), you unlock statutory damages ($750–$30,000 per work, up to $150,000 for willful infringement) and attorney's fees. This is what makes lawsuits actually viable.

---

## When to register copyright

**Always register if:**
- The work has serious commercial value (limited edition prints, licensed designs)
- The work is widely distributed (book illustrations, album artwork)
- You're licensing the work for products
- The work might be infringed (high-visibility public art, popular Instagram designs)

**Don't bother registering if:**
- You make hundreds of small unique works (paintings, ceramics) — registration cost doesn't pencil out
- The work has no commercial reproduction potential

**Group registration:** You can register up to 750 unpublished works as a single group for $85. Useful for photographers, illustrators with portfolios.

**How:** copyright.gov → eCO online system. Plan for 90 minutes the first time; faster after.

---

## What copyright doesn't protect

- **Ideas and styles.** You can't copyright "abstract landscapes" or "the technique of pouring resin."
- **Names, titles, short phrases.** "Stuart Range Ceramics" isn't copyrightable. (It might be trademarkable — see below.)
- **Facts and information.**
- **Useful articles** (the design of a chair) — though decorative elements can be.

---

## The DMCA takedown — your most useful tool

If someone is using your work without permission on a website, Instagram, Etsy listing, etc., you can send a **DMCA takedown notice** to the platform. The platform must remove the content or risk losing safe-harbor protection.

You don't need a lawyer to send one. Free templates online (or the Arts Commission can provide one).

What you need:
- Identification of the work
- Identification of the infringing material (URL)
- Statement that you own it
- Statement of good faith
- Your contact info
- Your signature

Most platforms (Instagram, Etsy, Shopify, Squarespace) have built-in DMCA forms. Use those.

---

## When DMCA isn't enough

If the infringer is:
- A company manufacturing products with your design — you need a cease & desist letter, then potentially a lawsuit
- Persistent and the platform won't act — same
- Profiting substantially from the work — same

This is where copyright registration matters. Without it, you can demand they stop. With it, you can collect.

## Trademark: for names and brands

Trademark protects **names, logos, and slogans** used in commerce. Different from copyright.

**You get common-law trademark rights** automatically by using a name in commerce in your region. "Stuart Range Ceramics" used in Kittitas County gives you common-law rights in Kittitas County.

**For broader protection**, register with the **USPTO**:
- $250–$350 per class (categories of goods/services)
- Takes 6–12 months to register
- Lasts indefinitely as long as you keep using and renewing

**Worth it for:**
- Studio names you've invested branding in
- Product lines
- Online stores with national reach

**Not worth it for:**
- A solo artist using their own name (you have common-law rights in your name automatically)
- A name you're not committed to long-term

## Practical protection habits

1. **Watermark images you post online** — visible enough to deter, invisible enough not to ugly-up the work. Add ©Your Name + year + IG handle.
2. **Use lower-res images for social, high-res only for buyers/galleries.**
3. **Embed copyright metadata** in image files (Photoshop, Lightroom).
4. **Reverse image search yourself periodically** (Google Images, TinEye). Catch infringement early.
5. **Keep originals or unedited files** — proof of authorship if disputed.
6. **Sign and date physical works** — basic but matters.

---

## Fair use — what someone else can do with your work

Fair use lets others use your work without permission in narrow cases: criticism, commentary, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, parody. The test:

1. Purpose (commercial vs educational)
2. Nature of the work
3. Amount used
4. Effect on the market

**An Instagram user posting your painting with critical commentary**: probably fair use.
**A t-shirt company printing your painting on shirts**: definitely not fair use.

- **CWU's library** has copyright reference resources for artists (and a librarian who knows IP).
- Local attorneys who handle IP for artists are limited — for serious infringement cases, expect a Seattle attorney ($400–$600/hour).

---

## Action items

1. Decide which of your works are worth registering. List them.
2. For unpublished portfolios, consider a group registration ($85 for up to 750 works).
3. Add ©YourName + year to anything you post online.
4. Set a quarterly reminder to reverse-image-search 3–5 of your most-shared works.
5. If you've been infringed, send a DMCA *this week*. Don't wait.

---

## Escalation triggers

- **Active commercial infringement** (someone selling your work) → IP attorney.
- **Trademark dispute** (another artist or business using a similar name) → trademark attorney.
- **Cease & desist sent to you** → attorney; don't reply without one.
- **Licensing deal worth >$5K** → attorney for the contract.
- **International infringement** → attorney with cross-border IP experience.
