# Module 15 — Health, Retirement, and Not Going Broke at 60

**Purpose:** Cover the long-game realities most artists avoid: health insurance, disability, retirement, and building a sustainable life around an art practice.
**Estimated time:** 30–40 minutes.
**Prerequisites:** Modules 01–07 helpful. This module assumes the artist is operating as a business.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist asks "what do I do about health insurance?"
- Artist is in their 30s/40s and realizing they have no retirement savings
- Artist had a health scare and got the bill
- Artist asks "is this sustainable for the next 30 years?"
- Artist is approaching Medicare age and unprepared

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"This is the module most artists avoid until it's a problem. Health, disability, retirement — the boring stuff that becomes urgent the moment something goes wrong. Self-employed artists have fewer safety nets than employees. Let me walk you through the ones you can set up, and the order they actually matter in."*

The framing: this isn't about wealth. It's about not being one bad year, one bad back, or one bad fall from losing everything you've built.

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **Do you have health insurance right now?** (How, what kind, what's it cost?)
2. **What's your age?** (Affects priorities and time horizons.)
3. **Are you the primary earner in your household, or supported by a spouse with benefits?**
4. **Do you have any retirement savings — IRA, 401(k) from a past job, anything?**
5. **What scares you most about your financial future?**

---

## Priority order for self-employed artists

1. **Health insurance** — non-negotiable.
2. **Emergency fund** — 3–6 months of expenses in a savings account.
3. **Disability insurance** — protects your income.
4. **Retirement contributions** — start small, automate.
5. **Life insurance** — only if others depend on your income.
6. **Long-term care planning** — for artists 50+.

---

## Health insurance for self-employed Kittitas artists

### Option A: WA Healthplanfinder (the marketplace)

- **What:** Washington's ACA marketplace. Most self-employed artists land here.
- **Open enrollment:** Nov 1 – Jan 15 most years.
- **Subsidies:** Income-based. Many artists qualify for substantial subsidies.
- **Plans:** Bronze (cheap monthly, high deductible), Silver (middle), Gold (more expensive, lower out-of-pocket).
- **Honest cost range in Kittitas County:** $200–$700/month after subsidy depending on age and income.
- **Premium tax credit:** You estimate income for the year; if you over-earn, you may owe back at tax time; if under-earn, you get refund.

### Option B: Spouse's employer plan

- If your spouse has employer coverage, getting on their plan is often cheaper than the marketplace.

### Option C: COBRA (if you just left a job)

- Lets you continue former employer's plan for 18 months, but you pay full premium (often $700+/month). Bridge only.

### Option D: Health share ministries

- **Not insurance.** Religious-affiliated cost-sharing. Cheaper monthly but with significant limitations.
- Generally **not recommended** for artists with any meaningful health needs.

### Tax deduction: self-employed health insurance

- You can **deduct your health insurance premiums** as an above-the-line deduction on your 1040.
- This often saves more than people realize. Talk to your CPA.

## Emergency fund

**Target:** 3–6 months of *expenses* (not income).

- Calculate your minimum monthly expenses: rent/mortgage, utilities, food, insurance, basic bills.
- Multiply by 3 (minimum) or 6 (better).
- Hold it in a **high-yield savings account** (Ally, Marcus, local credit union money market). ~4–5% APY currently.
- Don't touch it unless: medical emergency, major equipment failure, real income drop.

**For most Kittitas artists this is $8,000–$25,000.** It's a lot. Build it over 12–24 months.

---

## Disability insurance (the most overlooked)

If you can't make work — broken wrist for a painter, injured back for a sculptor, depression — your income stops. Social Security disability is hard to qualify for and slow.

- **Short-term disability** (3–12 months coverage): cheap, sometimes through Freelancers Union or membership orgs.
- **Long-term disability** (years to age 65): expensive ($75–250/month for self-employed), but a real safety net.
- Worth getting in your 30s–40s when it's cheaper.
- Brokers handle this, not direct.

## Retirement — the math nobody tells artists

The reality: Social Security alone for someone who earned moderate self-employment income their whole life is ~$1,400–$1,800/month at 67. That's not enough for most lifestyles.

**The fix is starting early and being consistent.**

### Option A: Roth IRA

- Contribute up to $7,000/year (2026, verify yearly).
- After-tax money, grows tax-free, withdraws tax-free in retirement.
- Best for artists with low to moderate current income (you're in a low tax bracket now).
- Open through Fidelity, Vanguard, or Schwab — no fee, no minimum to start.

### Option B: SEP-IRA (Simplified Employee Pension)

- Self-employed retirement account.
- Contribute up to **25% of net self-employment income**, max ~$70,000/year (2026, verify).
- Pre-tax (reduces this year's taxes), grows tax-deferred, taxed when withdrawn.
- Easier setup than Solo 401(k). Best for artists with $30K+ net profit who want to contribute meaningfully.

### Option C: Solo 401(k)

- Higher contribution potential than SEP at similar income levels.
- Allows Roth contributions and employee + employer pieces.
- Slightly more setup; some providers (Fidelity, Schwab) offer free ones.
- Best for higher-earning artists or those who want maximum tax-advantaged saving.

### The first-year game plan for a 35-year-old artist

- Year 1: Open a Roth IRA. Contribute $200/month ($2,400/year).
- Year 2: Same, increase to $300/month as income grows.
- Year 5: If net profit is $40K+, open a SEP-IRA and contribute 10–15% of net.
- Compound growth from 35 to 65 at 7% return turns $200/month into ~$240,000.

**The single biggest mistake: waiting until "you can afford it."** Starting at $50/month at 30 beats starting at $500/month at 45.

## Life insurance — only if others depend on you

- Single artist, no dependents: probably skip.
- Spouse and kids who'd be impacted by your death: yes. **Term life** (10–30 year terms) is cheap; $20–50/month for healthy adults under 50.
- Avoid "whole life" / "universal life" — sold as investment, mostly enriches the agent.

---

## Long-term care planning

For artists 50+: think about who cares for you if you have a stroke at 75 and can't paint or live independently.

- Long-term care insurance exists but is expensive.
- WA's WA Cares Fund (payroll-based) provides limited LTC benefits ($36,500 lifetime in 2026 dollars). Self-employed artists can opt in; verify current rules.
- Family conversations matter more than financial products at this stage.

---

## Mental health and the long game

Working artists report rates of anxiety and depression higher than the general population. Sustainability isn't just financial.

- WA Healthplanfinder plans cover mental health (ACA requirement).
- Artist Trust runs occasional artist wellness programs.
- CWU's counseling center sometimes offers community programs.
- The Arts Commission can refer to therapists who work with artists.

---

## Kittitas County specifics

- **Solarity Credit Union** offers self-employed friendly accounts including retirement.
- **Local insurance brokers** can be useful for finding marketplace plans and disability coverage; the Arts Commission has a vetted list.
- **CWU Wellness Center** programs occasionally extend to the broader community.
- Kittitas Valley Community Hospital handles routine care; major specialists are typically Yakima or Seattle.

---

## Action items

1. Get on health insurance if you're not. Don't go bare.
2. Calculate your minimum monthly expenses. Set an emergency fund target.
3. Open a Roth IRA this week. Even $50/month is a start.
4. If 35+, get a disability insurance quote.
5. If you're behind on retirement, run a "catch up" projection — what monthly contribution gets you to a reasonable retirement?
6. Check in on mental health honestly. Working artists are at higher risk.

---

## Escalation triggers

- Artist needs **comprehensive financial planning** → fee-only fiduciary advisor (NAPFA). Not a commission-based salesperson.
- Artist is **uninsured with a chronic condition** → urgent — get on a marketplace plan during next enrollment or qualifying event.
- Artist is **approaching retirement with insufficient savings** → financial planner + honest budget review.
- Artist has **inherited assets** (real estate, retirement accounts) → CPA + estate attorney.
- Artist is in a **mental health crisis** → 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline; local Comprehensive Healthcare in Ellensburg.
