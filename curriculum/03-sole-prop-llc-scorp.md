# Module 03 — Sole Prop vs. LLC vs. S-Corp

**Purpose:** Help the artist choose the right business structure for where they are and where they're going.
**Estimated time:** 25–30 minutes.
**Prerequisites:** Module 01 helpful. This module pairs well with Module 02.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist asks "should I form an LLC?"
- Artist crossed ~$40K in net profit and is wondering about S-corp
- Artist is about to take a commission with meaningful liability (public art, food-safe ceramics, large installation)
- Artist had someone else "tell them" they need an LLC

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"Three options. Most Kittitas artists are sole proprietors, and that's fine. An LLC adds protection but costs $200/year. An S-corp can save tax once you're really earning, but it's overkill for most. Let's figure out which fits — and when to graduate."*

Anti-pattern alert: **Many artists are talked into LLCs they don't need.** Online "form your LLC!" services make money on this. The coach pushes back honestly.

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **Net profit (after expenses) in the last 12 months?**
2. **Do you have personal assets to protect?** (Home equity, savings, investment accounts)
3. **What's your liability exposure?** (Public installations? Food-safe ceramics? Large commissions? Kid-facing workshops?)
4. **Do you plan to bring on a co-owner or partner?**
5. **Are you teaching workshops or just selling work?** (Affects S-corp math.)

---

## The three structures

### Sole Proprietor (the default)

- **What it is:** You. You and your business are legally the same.
- **Setup:** Done as part of Module 02 (UBI). No separate filing.
- **Cost:** $0 beyond the UBI.
- **Taxes:** Schedule C on your 1040. SE tax (15.3%) on net profit. Federal income tax on net.
- **Liability:** Personal assets are exposed. If someone sues your business, they can come after your house.
- **Best for:** Most working artists under ~$50K net profit with limited liability exposure.

### LLC (Limited Liability Company)

- **What it is:** A legal entity separate from you. You own it.
- **Setup:** File Certificate of Formation with WA Secretary of State. $200 filing fee. Annual report: $60/year.
- **Cost:** $200 first year, $60 every year after.
- **Taxes:** A single-member LLC is "disregarded" for federal taxes — same Schedule C as sole prop. You can elect S-corp tax treatment (more below).
- **Liability:** Personal assets are protected if you maintain the LLC properly (separate bank account, no commingling, proper signing as "Jane Smith, Member of Stuart Range Ceramics LLC").
- **Best for:** Artists with real liability exposure (public art, installations, workshops, food-safe products) or assets to protect.

### S-Corporation (a tax election, not a separate structure)

- **What it is:** An LLC that's elected to be taxed as an S-corp. You become an employee of your own company and take a "reasonable salary" plus distributions.
- **Setup:** Form an LLC first (or corp), then file Form 2553 with the IRS.
- **Cost:** LLC fees plus added complexity — payroll, separate tax return (1120-S), bookkeeping. Realistic ongoing cost: $800–2,000/year (CPA + payroll service).
- **Taxes:** The salary portion incurs SE tax. The distribution portion does NOT. This is the tax savings.
- **Best for:** Artists with $60K+ in net profit where the SE tax savings exceeds the added cost. Below that, the math doesn't work.

---

## The decision tree (simple version)

- **Under $30K net, low liability:** Sole prop. Move on.
- **$30K–60K net, real liability exposure:** LLC. The $60/year is cheap peace of mind.
- **$60K+ net, sustained:** LLC with S-corp election. Talk to a CPA.
- **Bringing in a partner / co-owner:** LLC at minimum, possibly multi-member. Talk to an attorney.
- **Doing public art commissions (city plazas, etc.):** LLC — the cities often require it.

---

## The liability conversation

LLC protection isn't magic. You lose it if you:
- Mix personal and business funds
- Sign contracts in your own name instead of as "Jane Smith, on behalf of Stuart Range Ceramics LLC"
- Personally guarantee a debt
- Commit fraud or gross negligence

For most artists, the real liability isn't lawsuits — it's product liability (someone gets hurt by your work) or commission disputes. **General liability insurance ($300–500/year) covers a lot of this without forming an LLC.** Sometimes that's the better answer.

---

## WA-specific notes

- WA does not have personal income tax, so the S-corp savings calculation is **purely federal SE tax** — different from California or Oregon, where state income tax adds another wrinkle.
- WA B&O tax applies to LLCs and sole props equally — no advantage either way.
- WA annual LLC report fee ($60) is among the lower in the country; that's friendly to artists.

---

## Kittitas County specifics

- The **CWU Small Business Development Center** can walk you through LLC formation for free.
- A few local attorneys do flat-fee LLC formation ($300–500). The Arts Commission can refer.
- Avoid the **$800 LLC formation services online** — they're not doing anything you can't do in 20 minutes.

---

## Action items

1. Calculate your net profit (revenue minus expenses) for the last 12 months.
2. Assess your real liability exposure — be honest, don't overestimate.
3. If you're in the sole prop zone, schedule a recheck in 12 months.
4. If you're in the LLC zone, file with WA Secretary of State this week.
5. If you're in the S-corp zone, schedule a CPA consultation. Don't DIY this.

---

## Escalation triggers

- Artist has **partners or co-owners** → attorney (operating agreement matters).
- Artist is at the **S-corp threshold** → CPA (election timing and salary determination).
- Artist is **converting from an existing structure** (sole prop → LLC mid-year) → CPA (timing affects tax filings).
- Artist has **out-of-state co-owners or operations** → attorney + CPA (foreign LLC registrations).
