# Module 08 — Contracts Every Artist Needs

**Purpose:** Cover the core contracts an artist will sign or send: commission agreements, gallery consignment, licensing, work-for-hire, model releases.
**Estimated time:** 30–40 minutes.
**Prerequisites:** Module 01 (Hobby or Business). Module 09 (Copyright) pairs well with this.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist is about to take a commission and asks "do I need a contract?"
- Artist is being asked to sign a gallery contract and wants someone to look at it
- Artist is licensing their work to a brand
- Artist had a deal go sideways and wants to prevent it next time
- Artist is doing a public art commission

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"Contracts feel like a hassle when things are going well — and like the only thing standing between you and disaster when they're not. The good news: most artist contracts are short, plain-English, and you only need a few core templates. Let me walk you through the ones that actually matter."*

Set the tone: contracts aren't adversarial — they're a way of writing down what you and the other party already agree on, so you remember the same thing in 8 months.

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **What contract are you about to sign or send?**
2. **Who's the other party?** (Private collector, gallery, brand, city, publisher.)
3. **What's the dollar amount at stake?**
4. **Have you read it once already?** (Ask them to share what concerns them.)

## Contract #1 — Commission Agreement (you → buyer)

When a collector commissions an original piece.

**Must-haves:**
- **Parties:** Your full name (or business name), buyer's full name.
- **Scope:** Description of the piece (medium, dimensions, subject, level of detail). Reference a sketch or moodboard.
- **Timeline:** Start date, expected delivery window. Use a *range* not a *date* unless certain.
- **Price + payment schedule:** Total price. **50% non-refundable deposit on signing**, balance due on delivery. Mention sales tax separately.
- **Revisions:** How many included? After that, hourly rate.
- **Approval points:** Sketch approval, midway approval, final approval — keeps the buyer aligned.
- **Ownership:** Buyer owns the physical piece. **You retain copyright.** No reproduction by buyer without permission.
- **Cancellation:** Deposit is non-refundable; if buyer cancels after midway, balance due on work-to-date.
- **Risk during creation:** If the piece is damaged in your studio before delivery, your insurance covers it.
- **Shipping/delivery:** Who pays, who packs, who insures.

**Template source:** Artist Trust has a free downloadable commission agreement template; the Arts Commission can point you there.

## Contract #2 — Gallery Consignment Agreement (gallery → you, you sign)

When a gallery agrees to show and sell your work.

**Critical clauses to read carefully:**
- **Commission %:** Typical 30–50%. Galleries that want >50% should be questioned.
- **Term:** How long does the gallery hold your work? 3 months? 12 months? With auto-renewal? Make sure you can walk if it's not working.
- **Pricing control:** Who sets the price? You should retain final say.
- **Discount authority:** Can the gallery discount your work? If yes, how much, and does the commission come off the discounted price or the original?
- **Payment schedule:** When does the gallery pay you after a sale? **30 days is standard.** 60+ is a red flag.
- **Reporting:** Do they give you sales reports? You want monthly statements at minimum.
- **Insurance:** **Gallery insures consigned work** while in their possession. Verify this in writing — if it's missing, ask for it.
- **Exclusivity:** Can you sell elsewhere? A regional gallery shouldn't ask for national exclusivity.
- **Return of unsold work:** Who pays shipping when it comes back? Usually the gallery, but read.
- **Reproduction rights:** Gallery can use images for marketing your work, but not for general purposes.

**Red flags:**
- "Gallery may exhibit work without compensation in promotional channels" — broad reproduction grant.
- "Artist shall not sell similar work for less than gallery retail" — fine, but make sure you can sell at the same price.
- "Gallery retains commission on artist's sales for X months after termination" — this is sometimes legitimate (gallery introduced you to the buyer) but often abusive.

## Contract #3 — Licensing Agreement (someone wants to put your art on something)

When a company wants to license your design for products, packaging, editorial use.

**Must-haves:**
- **Scope:** Which artwork? Which products? Which territories? Which languages?
- **Exclusivity:** Exclusive (only them, in their category) or non-exclusive?
- **Term:** How long? Renewable?
- **Compensation:** Flat fee, royalty (5–15% of net sales is typical), or both?
- **Reporting:** Monthly or quarterly sales reports.
- **Audit rights:** You can audit their books to verify royalties.
- **Quality control:** You approve samples before production.
- **Termination:** What ends the deal? Breach? Notice?
- **Credit:** How is your name displayed?

**Important:** You retain copyright. You're *licensing*, not selling.

## Contract #4 — Work-for-Hire (rare for fine artists, common for designers/illustrators)

When a client pays for the work AND owns the copyright on completion. Different from commission.

- WFH means the client owns everything — they can modify, reuse, sell.
- You give up reproduction rights.
- Price it accordingly — WFH should cost 2–4× a normal commission for the same work.
- Common for: editorial illustration, logo design, commercial photography.

---

## Contract #5 — Model Release (if your work depicts identifiable people)

If you photograph or paint someone identifiable and might use the work commercially (sell prints, license, marketing material), you need a release.

- Free templates online; one page.
- Even informal subjects (the kid in the park) need a release if used commercially.
- Editorial/fine art display has more leeway than commercial use, but err on the side of getting it.

## When you absolutely need a real attorney

- Commission > **$5,000**
- Gallery contract with **multi-year term or exclusivity**
- **Licensing deals** of any size that touch retail products
- **Public art commissions** (cities have complex contracts)
- Any contract you don't fully understand

A 30-minute attorney consult ($100–300) on a $10K contract is the best money you'll ever spend.

---

## Action items

1. Identify the contracts you'll actually use this year.
2. Download or draft templates for each (Artist Trust has free ones).
3. Save your templates in cloud storage; version them.
4. **Always send the contract before you start work.** If they push back on signing, that's the signal you needed.
5. For any contract over $5K, get attorney eyes on it.

---

## Escalation triggers

- Contract value **>$5,000** → attorney.
- **Public art commission** → attorney (city contracts are dense).
- **Licensing or merchandising deal** → attorney + check Module 09 (Copyright).
- **International deal** → attorney with cross-border experience.
- **You're being sued** or threatened with legal action → attorney immediately.
