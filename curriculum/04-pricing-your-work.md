# Module 04 — Pricing Your Work

**Purpose:** Give the artist a defensible pricing framework and dismantle the emotional baggage that makes most artists undervalue themselves.
**Estimated time:** 25–35 minutes.
**Prerequisites:** None, though Module 12 (Galleries) and Module 13 (Selling Online) build on this.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist asks "how do I price this?"
- Artist feels underpaid but doesn't know what to charge
- Artist is pricing a commission for the first time
- Artist is considering a price increase
- Artist is confused about gallery vs. direct pricing

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> *"Pricing is the question I get most, and it's the one with the most emotional weight. Let's separate the math from the feelings. By the end of this module, you'll have a defensible number for any piece — and a way to explain it to yourself."*

Most artists undervalue themselves. Not because the work isn't worth it — because they've never been taught a framework.

---

## Diagnostic questions

1. **What's your medium?** (Pottery, paintings, photography, jewelry, sculpture, performance, writing, design — different markets, different conventions.)
2. **Where do you currently sell?** (Direct, gallery, online, fairs, commissions, all of the above.)
3. **What's the price range of your work today?**
4. **What's bothering you about pricing right now?** Underpaid? Pricing something new? Considering a raise? Confused by gallery math?

The answer to #4 determines which framework to use.

---

## Framework A: Cost-plus (good for production work, jewelry, craft)

```
Price = (Materials + Time + Overhead) × (1 + Profit Margin)
```

- **Materials:** Actual cost of supplies for this piece.
- **Time:** Hours × your hourly rate. For Kittitas County, **$30–50/hour** is reasonable for skilled craft work; established artists go higher ($75–150).
- **Overhead:** ~30% of materials + time (covers studio, insurance, tools, software, marketing).
- **Profit margin:** 20–50% on top.

**Example:** $30 materials + (4 hrs × $40/hr) + 30% overhead ($57) + 30% margin = **$321**.

Watch-out: Most artists pay themselves $10–15/hour without realizing it. If your "hourly rate" is what's left over after costs, you're working below minimum wage.

---

## Framework B: Market-based (good for paintings, sculpture, fine art)

Compare what artists at your medium and career stage charge in your region.

**Painting square-inch pricing** (rough guide for Pacific NW market):
- Emerging artist: $1–5/sq in
- Mid-career: $5–25/sq in
- Established: $25–100/sq in
- Recognized name: $100+/sq in

**Sculpture:** Per pound + complexity premium, or per piece based on comparable artist tier.

**Photography:** Per print size, plus edition rarity premium.

This is the "what will the market bear" approach. Critical when you sell through galleries — the gallery's collector base has a price range in mind, and pricing outside it kills your placement.

---

## Framework C: Value-based (good for commissions, licensing)

Price the impact, not the time.

- A commissioned family portrait that hangs in a home for 50 years is worth more than the hours it took.
- A logo or pattern licensed to a regional brand is worth a percentage of what it'll earn the licensee (5–15% royalty range).
- A public art commission is priced on budget bracket, not hours.

Useful question: *"What's it worth to this client to have this exist?"*

---

## The gallery math gotcha (most artists miss this)

Galleries take **30–50% commission** on consignment.

- You sell direct for $500 → you net $500.
- You sell through a 50% gallery for $500 → you net $250.

**This means gallery prices and direct prices must be the same.** If you sell direct for $500 and gallery retail is also $500, the gallery nets $250 and you net $250. Most artists don't realize they should be selling direct at gallery-retail price (so they net the full $500 direct).

**Rule of thumb:** Set your "retail" price assuming gallery sale, then sell direct at the same number. Never undercut your gallery — they'll drop you.

## Pricing a commission (the script)

1. Get the brief in writing. Size, medium, complexity, deadline, usage.
2. Estimate hours conservatively (artists always underestimate by 30–50%).
3. Calculate cost-plus.
4. Add a **commission premium** of 25–50%. Commissions are riskier (client revisions, deadline pressure).
5. Quote the client a number that includes a **50% deposit, non-refundable**.
6. Put it all in a contract (see Module 08).

---

## When to raise prices

- You're consistently sold out / have a waitlist
- It's been 18+ months since your last raise
- Your costs (materials, studio rent) have risen
- You won a notable show, award, or commission
- A peer at your tier is charging more for comparable work

Raise prices on new work, not existing inventory. 10–20% increments are normal. Don't apologize for it.

---

## Kittitas County specifics

- **Buskers in the Burg** foot traffic supports work in the **$30–300 range**. Higher-priced originals move slowly at festivals; bring smaller works or prints for volume and a few hero pieces for portfolio.
- **Kittitas County Fair** is a different audience — agricultural community, regional tourism. Modest price points do well.
- **Gallery One consignment terms** vary — confirm directly. Common range is 35–50% commission.
- **Suncadia & area lodging** sometimes buy mid-priced originals ($300–$1,500) for guest rooms; pricing should reflect a hospitality-buyer perspective (no per-sq-in math).
- **Commissions in Kittitas County** typically range $400–$2,500 for visual artists. Don't undersell — the buyer chose you specifically.

---

## Action items

1. Calculate cost-plus for your last sale. Were you under or over?
2. Set a baseline hourly rate. Write it down.
3. If you sell through any gallery, audit your direct prices — are they the same as gallery retail?
4. Pick one piece you've been undervaluing. Reprice it.
5. Draft a written commission pricing checklist you can reuse.

---

## Escalation triggers

- Artist is doing **wholesale to retailers** (different math entirely — wholesale = retail ÷ 2.2 or so).
- Artist is negotiating a **licensing deal worth >$5K** — they need an attorney for the contract.
- Artist's pricing is **wildly off market** in either direction — may need pricing-focused mentor (refer to Artist Trust).
