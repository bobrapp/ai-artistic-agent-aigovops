# Module 01 — Hobby or Business?

**Purpose:** Help the artist self-diagnose whether what they're doing is legally a business, and what changes if so.
**Estimated time:** 15–20 minutes.
**Prerequisites:** None. This is often the first module.

---

## When to pull this module

- Artist says "it's just a side thing" but is selling regularly
- Artist asks if they need to "report this" or "form a business"
- Artist is losing money and wondering if they can deduct it
- Artist just had their first $1K+ year

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

Frame the goal: this isn't about labels, it's about which tax treatment fits — because the rules are different. Reassure them: "There's no wrong answer; we're just matching reality to the right category."

> *"The IRS doesn't care what you call it. It cares whether you're trying to make money or doing it for love. Both are valid — but the tax rules are different. Let's figure out which side of the line you're on."*

---

## Diagnostic questions

Ask in order:

1. **Roughly how much have you sold in the last 12 months?**
2. **Is that more, less, or about the same as you spent on materials and supplies?**
3. **Do you actively try to sell — pricing, marketing, showing up at fairs, taking commissions?**
4. **Would you keep doing this if it never made money?** (honest answer is fine)
5. **How long have you been at this?**

---

## The IRS test (in plain English)

The IRS uses nine factors. You don't need to pass all nine — they look at the whole picture. The big ones for artists:

1. **Do you operate it like a business?** (Bank account, records, contracts, pricing strategy)
2. **Do you act like you intend to profit?** (Marketing, fair applications, website, social media)
3. **Have you made profit in 3 of the last 5 years?** (Strong presumption of business)
4. **Do you have the expertise or are you developing it?** (Classes, conferences, formal training)
5. **Are losses explainable?** (Startup phase, market downturn — not "I just enjoy it")

**Practical rule:** If you sold $500+ in a year and you act like you're trying to sell, the IRS sees a business. Selling $50 of holiday-card prints to your aunt does not.

---

## What changes if you're a business

**Reporting:**
- File Schedule C with your 1040
- Track all income and all expenses

**Deductions (this is the big one):**
- Materials, supplies, framing, packaging
- Studio space (home or rented) — square footage method
- Mileage to fairs, galleries, suppliers (current IRS rate: ~$0.67/mile, verify yearly)
- Classes, workshops, conferences, professional development
- Software (Adobe, Squarespace, Etsy fees)
- Insurance (liability, studio property)
- Marketing (website, business cards, ads)
- A reasonable portion of phone and internet

**Costs:**
- Federal self-employment tax (15.3%) on net profit
- WA Business License (UBI), ~$90 to start
- WA B&O tax (gross receipts; small business credit often zeros it out at low revenue)
- Sales tax collection on retail sales

---

## What stays the same if it's a hobby

- Income is still reported (line 8j on Schedule 1, "Activity not for profit")
- **You cannot deduct expenses against hobby income.** This is the post-2017 trap that bites people.
- No SE tax
- No B&O or business license required (but selling at a fair may still trigger temporary registration)

---

## The honest gray zone

Many artists hover here. The coach should:

- **Not tell them they "have to" be a business** if it's genuinely small and casual
- **Strongly suggest the business path** if they're selling $1K+, taking commissions, or losing money on materials they want to deduct
- **Note the cost of doing nothing**: if the IRS reclassifies you later, you can owe back taxes plus penalties

---

## Kittitas County specifics

- Selling at the **Kittitas County Fair** or **Buskers in the Burg** technically triggers WA temporary sales tax collection obligations even for hobbyists. Festival organizers usually issue a sub-permit; ask them.
- **Gallery One** generally won't sign a consignment artist who isn't operating as a business — they need someone they can issue a 1099 to.
- **Suncadia / area lodging** purchases will issue a 1099 if you cross $600 in a year.

---

## Action items

1. Look at your last 12 months: revenue, expenses, net.
2. Honestly answer: am I trying to make money, or making money incidentally?
3. If you're a business: move to Module 02 (Registering in Washington).
4. If you're a hobby: keep simple records and revisit in 12 months.
5. Either way: open a separate bank account. (Cheapest insurance for clarity later.)

---

## Escalation triggers

- Artist had **major losses 3+ years in a row** and wants to deduct them → CPA needed (hobby-loss reclassification risk).
- Artist received an **IRS letter** about the activity → CPA immediately.
- Artist is unsure because of **complicated income mix** (day job, royalties, inherited art being sold) → CPA.
