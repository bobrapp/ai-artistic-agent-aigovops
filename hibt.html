Behind the Build

How I Built the Kittitas Artist Business Coach

The entire project — an AI-powered business advisory system for rural artists, a 16-module curriculum, an 8-page website, a self-running demo, and a pilot recruitment pipeline — was built in a single conversation with an AI agent. Here's how.

🎨
Dr. Don Solberg
Kittitas County Arts Commission · May 2026

The premise

Kittitas County, Washington — home to about 480 working artists across Ellensburg, Cle Elum, Roslyn, and the surrounding valley — had a gap. Artists here are brilliant at their craft. The business side? Taxes, contracts, pricing, galleries, grants, insurance — that's where they're alone.

A CPA charges $200/hour. A consultant in Seattle is a 90-minute drive. Most artists piece it together from blog posts and friends who learned the hard way.

The idea: what if we built a 24/7 AI business advisor that actually knows Kittitas County? Not a generic chatbot. A coach that knows Gallery One's consignment terms, Washington's B&O tax rules, Buskers in the Burg deadlines, and the difference between a sole proprietorship and an LLC at $14,000 in pottery sales.

The constraint that shaped everything: This is a rural arts commission with a tiny budget. No content production studio. No paid consultants on retainer. No course platform to maintain. The entire system had to run on ~$50/month and ~1 hour/month of volunteer maintenance.

The architecture

The system has three layers:

1. The AI Coach (the brain)

A custom-configured AI agent running on Anthropic's Claude, with a detailed system prompt covering:

  • Washington state tax law (B&O, sales tax, UBI registration)
  • Business formation guidance (sole prop vs. LLC vs. S-corp thresholds)
  • Kittitas County-specific knowledge (venues, institutions, professionals, deadlines)
  • A four-layer AI protection framework (Protect, Detect, Fight, Advocate)
  • Pricing methodologies, contract templates, grant writing guidance
  • Appropriate disclaimers and handoff to licensed professionals

2. The Living Curriculum (the knowledge)

Sixteen modules covering every aspect of the business of being an artist, from "Hobby or Business?" to "Protecting Your Work from AI." Each module is a guided conversation — not a recorded video or a PDF. The coach walks you through, asks about your situation, and gives next steps that fit.

3. The website (the front door)

A static site on GitHub Pages — nine HTML pages, zero frameworks, zero build tools. Every page is hand-crafted HTML + CSS + vanilla JS, styled in a warm editorial aesthetic with Fraunces serif headings and a terracotta accent palette.

The build timeline

Everything was built through iterative conversation. Each step was a request, a review, and a refinement:

Session 1

System architecture & prompt engineering

Designed the agent's identity, knowledge boundaries, tone, and disclaimer framework. Wrote the 12,500-word system prompt covering taxes, contracts, pricing, local knowledge, and AI protection.

Session 2

Homepage & core pages

Built the main landing page, "How it works" section, chat preview, curriculum grid, and local resources section. Established the visual design system.

Session 3

Board pitch deck & roadmap

Created a full slide presentation for the KCAC board and a multi-phase strategic roadmap showing how the coach improves over time.

Session 4

Living Curriculum modules

Wrote all 16 curriculum modules as detailed conversation scripts. Each module is 2,000–4,000 words of guided dialogue, decision trees, and WA-specific examples.

Session 5

AI Protection Framework

Designed the four-layer shield (Protect, Detect, Fight, Advocate). Added Module 16 to the curriculum. Built the dedicated protect.html page explaining the framework.

Session 6

Arts directory & showcase demo

Built a filterable directory of 50+ Kittitas County arts entries and a self-running 10-section interactive demo with typewriter chat animations.

Session 7

Pilot recruitment & sign-up

Created three versions of a recruitment email, a pilot sign-up form with mailto: submission, and wired all CTAs across the site.

Session 8

E2E testing & polish

Full browser testing at desktop and mobile viewports. Fixed bugs (module names, dead links, missing nav). Added shared navigation, hamburger menu, and cross-page linking.

What makes it different

It's agent-first, not content-first

Traditional artist business education creates content: videos, PDFs, webinars, worksheets. This project creates a coach. The content is generated on the fly, tailored to the specific artist's medium, revenue, location, and stage. There's nothing to film, nothing to update manually, nothing that goes stale.

It fights for artists, not against them

The biggest trust barrier for AI in the arts world is that most AI tools threaten artists — generating art, scraping training data, competing for commissions. This coach flips that narrative. Module 16 teaches artists to protect their work from AI scraping. The four-layer shield (Protect, Detect, Fight, Advocate) is front and center in the marketing. Leading with protection earns the trust needed to introduce AI as an ally.

It knows the county by name

When it tells you about a consignment program, it means Gallery One. When it tells you where to apply for a state grant, it means ArtsWA — with the deadlines specific to our region. When it calculates sales tax, it uses Kittitas County's actual combined rate of ~8.2%. This local grounding is what makes the advice actionable instead of generic.

It costs almost nothing to run

Estimated monthly cost: ~$50. No salaries, no office hours, no course platform subscriptions. One well-configured agent serves every artist in the county at near-zero marginal cost. The Commission volunteers ~1 hour/month to refresh seasonal info (grant deadlines, tax changes, fair dates).

The AI governance angle

This project is also an experiment in AI governance for public-interest organizations (what we call "AiGovOps"). The question isn't just "can AI help artists?" — it's "how should a public arts commission deploy AI responsibly?"

Key governance decisions baked into the system:

  • Transparency: The coach always identifies as an AI. It never pretends to be human.
  • Scope limits: It gives general business education, not legal or tax advice. It routes to licensed professionals for anything requiring credentials.
  • Privacy: Artist conversations are private. No data is sold or used for training.
  • Accountability: The Arts Commission reviews and maintains the system prompt. Community feedback drives curriculum updates.
  • Artist protection: The AI actively helps artists fight AI misuse — it doesn't just avoid harm, it advocates for artists' rights.

What's next

The pilot launches summer 2026 with 10–25 Kittitas County artists. If it works — if artists actually use it, if the business outcomes improve, if the trust model holds — the template is replicable. Any rural arts commission with a small budget could deploy a version configured for their county, their tax rules, their venues, their professionals.

The code is open. The architecture is documented. The conversation that built it is the proof that you don't need a dev team or a six-figure budget to create something genuinely useful.
