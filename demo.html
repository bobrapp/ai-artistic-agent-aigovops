Tax & Compliance
M

Maria Delgado

Ceramic potter  ·  Ellensburg, WA  ·  First-time fair vendor

"I got a booth at the Kittitas County Fair this August. Do I have to collect sales tax on my pottery? I've never done this before."

M
Maria
I just got approved for a booth at the Kittitas County Fair! Really exciting, but now I'm panicking a little. Do I need to collect sales tax on my pottery? I have no idea where to even start.
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

Congratulations on the booth — that's a big deal! And yes, you will need to collect sales tax on your pottery sales in Washington. The good news is it's very manageable once you know the steps, so let's walk through it.

The basic rule: Washington State requires that you collect sales tax on retail sales of physical goods — your handmade ceramics absolutely count. The rate is destination-based, meaning you use the rate for where the customer takes possession of the item. At the Kittitas County Fair in Ellensburg, that rate is 8.2%.

Quick check: Do you already have a Washington UBI number and a Reseller Permit? If not, those are your first two steps — both free through the Department of Revenue.

One thing worth knowing about county fairs specifically: many fair organizers have a master sales tax permit and can issue a temporary sub-permit to vendors under it. This simplifies your reporting. I'd strongly recommend emailing the Fair office to ask — get their answer in writing so you're clear on exactly what they handle and what you handle.

I'm an AI coach, not a CPA — for your specific situation, talking to a tax professional or calling the WA Department of Revenue directly (they have a surprisingly helpful helpline) is always a good idea.

M
Maria
I do have a UBI number — I got it when I started selling online. What does "destination-based" mean exactly? And what do I actually do with the tax I collect?
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

Great — having your UBI number already puts you ahead of the curve.

Destination-based just means the tax rate is tied to wherever the buyer takes the item home, not where you're based. So if someone from Ellensburg buys a mug at your booth, you charge 8.2%. If someone from Seattle buys something and takes it home, technically you'd use Seattle's rate — but in practice at a fair where customers walk away with their purchase right there, you'll use the fair's local rate for virtually every transaction. Clean and simple.

What you do with the money you collect:

  • Add 8.2% to each sale (or price it in — either works)
  • Keep that collected tax in a separate mental bucket — it's not your income
  • Track your gross fair sales separately from your regular sales
  • Report and remit it on your regular B&O and retail sales tax return through My DOR (the WA Dept of Revenue online portal)
Practical tip: If the Fair issues you a sub-permit, they may handle the tax remittance themselves and you just report your sales to them. That's why confirming with the Fair office in writing is so important — you don't want to double-remit.

The Fair organizers have handled hundreds of vendors, so don't be shy about asking them directly. Would you like help drafting that email to them?

Maria
That would actually be really helpful! Also — should I be thinking about anything else before the fair? Like, is there paperwork I might be missing?

Here's a quick pre-fair checklist for you:

  • UBI number — ✓ you've got this
  • Reseller Permit — get this if you're buying raw materials wholesale (it lets you purchase clay, glazes, etc. without paying sales tax on supplies)
  • Sub-permit confirmation — email the Fair office, ask: "Will I be selling under the Fair's master permit, or do I need my own temporary retail permit?"
  • Fair sales tracking — keep a simple tally by day so your B&O filing is easy after the event
  • Square or cash box — consider how you'll handle payments; Square has a simple tax rate setting so it calculates automatically

Pricing & Business Strategy
J

Jake Ostrowski

Oil painter  ·  Cle Elum, WA  ·  Self-taught, 8 years painting

"Honestly I've just been making up prices or giving work away. People keep telling me I'm too cheap but I don't know how to actually figure out what to charge."

J
Jake
I've been painting oils for about 8 years. I mostly sell at local events or through word of mouth. My prices are kind of all over the place — like $150 for something I spent 40 hours on. My friend says I'm severely undercharging but I don't know how to think about this properly.
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

Your friend is right — and I say that with real warmth, not judgment. $150 for 40 hours of work comes out to less than $4 an hour before you even account for materials. That's not a price, that's a donation.

Here's the thing: underpricing doesn't just hurt you financially — it subtly signals to buyers that your work isn't worth much. So getting this right is actually an act of self-respect and professionalism. Let's fix it.

Start with the cost-plus method. It gives you a rational floor — a price below which you simply don't go. Here's how it works with a real example:

Example: A 16×20 landscape, 20 hours of work

Materials (canvas, paints, varnish): $40
Your time (20 hrs × $25/hr living wage): $500
Overhead at 20% (studio, utilities, tools): +$108
Profit margin at 30%: +$194
Floor price: ~$842

That might feel like a big jump from $150, but notice — this is the floor, not the ceiling. Your work, your experience, and your market determine whether you price above this.

J
Jake
Okay that's kind of eye-opening. But I'm nervous nobody will buy at that price. How do I know if that's realistic for this area?
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

That's exactly the right follow-up question. Cost-plus tells you your floor; market comparison tells you your ceiling and helps you gauge what buyers in your category are actually paying.

How to do a market comparison:

  • Look at artists in the Pacific Northwest with similar experience (5–12 years), working in oil, at a similar scale to your work
  • Check galleries like Larson Gallery in Yakima, Davidson Galleries in Seattle, and regional arts councils for listed prices
  • Browse Artwork Archive or Saatchi Art filtering by "Pacific Northwest oil painting" — note what sizes sell at what prices
  • Locally: Gallery One in Ellensburg is a great reference point for what the Central WA market bears

What you'll likely find: experienced PNW oil painters working at the 16×20 scale are regularly pricing in the $600–$1,200 range. Your cost-plus number of ~$842 fits right in that market range. That's not coincidence — it's the market pricing in what the work actually costs to make.

The hardest truth: if your prices are too low, some buyers actually trust your work less. Price is a signal of value. Raising your prices can literally increase demand.

Start with a modest increase — say 40–50% — and see how the market responds. You don't have to jump to full cost-plus overnight.

J
Jake
What about my older pieces that I already have listed online at lower prices? Do I just change all of them?
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

Yes — but you can do it thoughtfully. A few options:

  • Just raise them. Most buyers don't remember your old prices, and a quiet update is totally normal. No announcement needed.
  • "Last piece at this price" framing. If you have a newsletter or social following, you can run a brief "studio sale" on older inventory at the old price, then announce new pricing going forward. This creates a sales event and explains the change in one move.
  • Don't grandfather indefinitely. A few people may have bookmarked an old piece. If they come back and it's now $700 instead of $150, most will understand — and a few will buy faster because they don't want to miss it again.

One more thing I want to say: you've been making work for eight years. That expertise and those hours have real value. Charging appropriately isn't greed — it's what allows you to keep making work. The world needs more of what you do.

If you want, I can help you build a simple pricing sheet you can use going forward for new pieces at different sizes and complexity levels.

Contracts & Legal
D

Dani Reyes

Mixed media artist  ·  Ellensburg, WA  ·  Emerging artist, 3 years

"Gallery One reached out about a consignment show this fall. They sent a contract and I don't really know what I'm looking at. Is this normal? Should I sign it?"

D
Dani
Gallery One asked me to be part of their fall show! I'm so excited but also a little scared. They sent a consignment agreement. Honestly I don't know what a "normal" contract looks like. How do I know if this is okay?
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

First — Gallery One is a great opportunity, and being asked to show there is a real recognition of your work. They've been part of the Ellensburg arts community for a long time and generally have artist-friendly terms. That said: always read the contract. Every one. No exceptions.

Here's what to look for in any consignment agreement:

  • Commission rate. 40–50% is the standard range for Pacific Northwest galleries. If they're asking for more than 50%, that's worth questioning.
  • Payment timeline. You should be paid within 30 days of a sale. If the contract says 60–90 days or doesn't specify, push back.
  • Insurance. The gallery should insure your work while it's in their care — at replacement value, not purchase price. If this isn't in the contract, ask them to add it.
  • Term length. 3–12 months with mutual termination rights (either party can end with 30 days notice) is healthy and fair.
  • Pricing control. You set prices. The gallery may propose discounts for collectors or opening night, but you should have final approval on any discount over a certain threshold — typically 10–15%.
  • Exclusivity. Local exclusivity (you won't show the same pieces elsewhere in Ellensburg during the term) is reasonable. National exclusivity for a regional gallery is overreach — don't agree to that.
D
Dani
What are the red flags I should watch out for? Like, what would make me walk away?
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

Great question to ask before you sign anything. Here are the clear red flags:

  • Upfront fees of any kind. Legitimate galleries make money when you make money. If they're asking you to pay for hanging, marketing, or a "gallery fee," walk away — that's a vanity gallery model.
  • Indefinite term. A contract with no end date or no mutual termination clause leaves your work in limbo indefinitely. Don't sign it.
  • No insurance language. If the contract doesn't mention who's responsible for damage or loss, your work is unprotected. Ask for an explicit clause.
  • Gallery sets prices. You are always the pricing authority on your own work. A gallery that insists on controlling your prices is a gallery that doesn't respect the artist relationship.
  • Broad exclusivity. Watch for language like "exclusive rights to represent your work" without clear geographic and time limits.
For what it's worth: Gallery One has been around long enough that I'd be surprised if their contract has serious red flags. But knowing what healthy terms look like protects you in every future relationship, not just this one.

One more thing — I'd recommend having an attorney review before you sign. The Arts Commission maintains a referral list of attorneys who work with artists in the region, often at reduced rates. It's worth one hour of legal time before you commit your work for a season.

I'm an AI coach, not a lawyer. Nothing I've shared here is legal advice — it's a starting framework for reading contracts, not a substitute for professional review.

D
Dani
This is so helpful. Can I negotiate terms if I don't like something, or is that weird to do with a gallery?
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

Not only is it not weird — it's expected, and it signals that you're a professional who takes your practice seriously. Galleries deal with contracts all the time. A polite, specific ask ("Could we add a 30-day payment term and insurance language?") is completely normal.

How to approach it:

  • Lead with enthusiasm — you want to show. "I'm really excited about this opportunity and want to make sure we both feel good about the agreement."
  • Be specific, not adversarial. Ask about one or two things, not a dozen.
  • Get any changes in writing — a signed addendum or revised contract, not just a verbal "sure, no problem."

Most galleries would rather make a small adjustment than lose a good artist. And if a gallery reacts badly to a respectful, reasonable ask? That tells you something important about what the working relationship will feel like.

You've got this, Dani. This show is going to be wonderful — now you'll also walk in knowing your work is protected.

Financial Planning
S

Sam Whitfield

Woodworker & furniture maker  ·  Ellensburg, WA  ·  Net profit ~$35K/year

"I'm 42. I have literally zero retirement savings. I know that's bad. Am I screwed?"

S
Sam
I'm 42 years old and I have zero retirement savings. I've been so focused on keeping the business alive that I just never did it. I know I'm behind. How bad is it?
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

You're not screwed. I want to say that clearly before anything else: you are not screwed. You have 23 years before a traditional retirement age. That is meaningful time, and what you do in the next 12 months matters far more than the last 20 years of not starting.

Here's the honest picture: yes, starting at 42 means you'll need to be intentional. But "intentional" doesn't mean dramatic — it means consistent. Small amounts, started now, do real work over time.

Let me give you a priority order that makes sense for someone in your situation:

  1. Emergency fund first. Before retirement, make sure you have 3–6 months of living expenses in liquid savings. For most Kittitas artists, that's roughly $12,000–$18,000. Without this, any retirement savings you build will just get raided the first time the truck needs a repair.
  2. Open a Roth IRA. Once your emergency fund is in place, this is your primary retirement tool as a self-employed person.
  3. SEP-IRA when income grows. If your net profit climbs above $40K, a SEP-IRA lets you contribute much more.
S
Sam
I've heard of a Roth IRA but I don't really understand it. How much would I actually end up with if I opened one now?
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

A Roth IRA is a retirement account where you contribute after-tax dollars, and then your investments grow completely tax-free. When you withdraw in retirement, you pay no taxes on any of the growth. For most self-employed people, it's the single best retirement tool available.

The math for your situation:

If you contribute $200/month starting at 42, invested in a basic index fund averaging 7% annual return:

By age 65 (23 years): approximately $150,000 — tax-free.

That $200/month costs you $2,400/year, or about $46/week.

Here's the number I really want you to sit with: $200/month from 42 to 65 produces roughly the same outcome as waiting until 52 and contributing $550/month. Time is worth more than contribution size.

The 2025 Roth IRA contribution limit is $7,000/year for people under 50 — you don't have to max it. Start with what you can actually sustain. $100/month is better than zero.

S
Sam
Okay you're actually making me feel better about this. Where do I open one? I don't know any financial advisors and I'm not sure I trust banks.
KC
Kittitas Artist Business Coach

Your instinct to be thoughtful about where you put your money is healthy. A few solid options:

  • Fidelity or Vanguard — Both are nonprofit-structured or low-fee institutions that have been around for decades. Opening a Roth IRA online takes about 20 minutes. You can start with a simple target-date index fund and automate a monthly contribution. No advisor required, no annual fees.
  • Locally: Solarity Credit Union (Ellensburg) offers IRA products and has staff who can sit down with you in person if you prefer that. Credit unions are member-owned, which aligns well with the values of most independent artists I talk to.
  • If you want a human advisor — look for a fee-only fiduciary CFP. "Fee-only" means they don't earn commissions, so their advice isn't influenced by what they sell. NAPFA.org has a directory.
The single biggest mistake I see is waiting until you can "afford it." $50/month at 42 is genuinely better than $500/month at 55. Open the account this week, set up a $100 automatic transfer, and adjust upward as your business grows.

And as your woodworking income grows — if you get net profit consistently above $40K — come back and let's talk about the SEP-IRA. It lets you shelter up to 25% of net profit, which is a powerful tool for a self-employed craftsperson.

I'm an AI coach, not a financial advisor. These are general educational frameworks — for personalized advice, a fee-only fiduciary CFP is worth every penny of a one-time consultation.
